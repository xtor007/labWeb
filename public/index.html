Orbital speed

The first cosmic speed (circular speed) is the minimum (for a given height above the planet's surface) horizontal speed that must be given to an object in order for it to move in a circular orbit around the planet.

The escape velocity from Earth's surface is about 7.9 km/s.

In the inertial reference frame, only one force will act on an object moving in a circular orbit around the Earth - the force of gravity of the Earth. In this case, the movement of the object will be neither uniform nor uniformly accelerated. This happens because the speed and acceleration (the values ​​are not scalar, but vector) in this case do not satisfy the conditions of uniformity / uniform acceleration of motion - that is, motion with a constant (in magnitude and direction) speed / acceleration.

Indeed, the velocity vector will be constantly directed tangentially to the Earth's surface, and the acceleration vector will be perpendicular to it to the center of the Earth, while these vectors will constantly change their direction as they move along the orbit. Therefore, in the inertial frame of reference, such a motion is often called "motion in a circular orbit with a constant modulus of speed."
