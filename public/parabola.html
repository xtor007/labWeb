Parabola speed

The second space speed (parabolic speed, release speed, escape speed) is the smallest speed that must be given to an object starting from the surface of a celestial body (for example, a spacecraft), the mass of which is negligible compared to the mass of a celestial body, for (for example, a planet) overcoming the gravitational attraction of this celestial body and leaving the closed orbit around it.

The escape velocity from Earth's surface is about 11.2 km/s.

To obtain the formula for the second cosmic speed, it is convenient to reverse the problem - to ask what speed the body will receive on the planet's surface if it falls on it from infinity. Obviously, this is exactly the speed that must be given to the body on the surface of the planet in order to bring it beyond the limits of its gravitational influence.

The second cosmic speed is called parabolic because the bodies, which at the start have a speed exactly equal to the second cosmic speed, move along a parabola relative to the celestial body. However, if a little more energy is imparted to the body, its trajectory ceases to be a parabola and becomes a hyperbola. If a little less, then it turns into an ellipse. In general, they are all conical sections.

Meaning for different planets(km/s):

  • Mercury - 4.3

  • Earth - 11.2
  • Moon - 2.4
